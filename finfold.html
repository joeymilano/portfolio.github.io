Why this problem is worth solving

"A marketing manager runs six platforms. She writes the same product story six times — each version slightly worse, slightly off-brand. By the time she posts to LinkedIn, she's exhausted."

Content teams working across Chinese and global platforms face a compounding problem: every channel has its own tone, format, and algorithmic preference. WeChat rewards structured long-form prose. Xiaohongshu rewards aesthetic, first-person storytelling. TikTok rewards a hook in the first 1.5 seconds. LinkedIn rewards professional authority. These aren't just format differences — they're persona differences.

The status-quo workflow is write-once, paste-everywhere, manually rewrite, lose consistency. General AI tools don't help structurally — they're blank-canvas chatbots that assume you already know what "native" means on each platform.

The hard design problems

How do you design for AI output that changes personality on every platform — without the UI feeling like a spreadsheet?

Platform persona vs. brand voice

Each platform has a distinct register — emotional tone, sentence length, emoji use, CTA style. The AI must adopt the platform persona while staying inside the brand's defined voice and prohibited language. Design decision: guardrails are enforced silently inside the generation prompt, so users only ever see compliant output — never the constraint machinery. Violations surface in aggregate, not inline, which keeps the writing surface calm.

The three-column workbench

The workbench is the product's core surface. The journey is: (1) input brief + asset → (2) choose platforms, persona, and goal → (3) review the generated kit. A single scrolling form collapses this into a linear task with no spatial orientation. The three-column layout externalizes the mental model — left anchors intent, middle anchors context, right surfaces output. Borrowed from IDE layout theory: you don't read left-to-right, you triangulate.

Agent state, made scannable

The Agents surface runs recurring automations — gap scouting, scheduled production, QA, publishing. Workflows carry state (idle, scanning, ready, in review, paused) and that state must be triageable at a glance. Design decision: status uses color + icon + micro-label, and the running-state indicator changes the card's emphasis. You can spot a stalled workflow without opening a single one.

Designing for output variance

AI output is non-deterministic — the same brief run twice produces different copy. That's a feature, but it creates a UX expectation problem. Users need to regenerate one platform without losing the others. Design decision: each output is independently editable and regenerable, and a lock keeps the ones you like while you refresh the rest. Unpredictability turns from anxiety into exploration.

Agents — the automation layer

A self-driving content loop: agents read the dashboard, detect platform gaps, invoke workbench production, run brand-guardrail QA, and queue work for human approval. State-coded cards and a live run log make the automation legible. The design insight: enterprise buyers don't buy AI — they buy predictability. The Agents screen is built to feel like an ops console, not a wizard.

Guardrails — the brand immune system

A rules grid where brand managers define what the AI can and can't say: prohibited claims, CTA standards, persona-voice constraints, and commercial-asset license records. Each rule is bound straight into the workbench generation prompt. The principle: guardrails aren't censorship — they're institutional memory made machine-readable, and they sit at the top level of the nav as a deliberate trust signal.

Calendar — the publishing rhythm

A weekly card grid of scheduled and published posts per channel. Diagnostics surface under-scheduled platforms ("Bilibili and Instagram each have only one post in a scheduled state"), topic conflicts, and review bottlenecks. The principle: the calendar is a pressure gauge, not a passive record — empty days carry visual weight, full days feel earned.

Five tiers — Free, Starter, Creator, Pro, Team — mapped to monthly kit volume and platform access. "The platform is the medium. The brand is the message. The AI is the translator. The designer's job is to make the translation invisible."

1. The output board is an editing surface, not a preview. Early prototypes showed read-only previews with a separate edit step. Writers wanted to tweak inline, not open a new screen — so "preview" and "edit" collapse into one surface. Click anywhere in the copy to edit it. This is the single biggest time-to-publish win in the product.

2. Guardrails are a first-class citizen, not a settings page. Most AI writers bury safety rules in a menu. Here, Guardrails is a top-level nav item, equal to Workbench and Calendar. That structural choice signals — before anyone types a word — that brand safety is a core product value, not an afterthought.

3. Streaming output is performance design. Platforms generate in parallel, and each card renders token-by-token as its stream arrives. Technically it's the same total latency as a bulk load — but the experience turns a wait into a show, and the product simply feels fast.

What I learned building an AI product end-to-end

"Working directly at the API seams changed how I design. You stop drawing idealized AI flows and start designing the real handoffs between intent, generation, review, and recovery."

1. AI products need a trust architecture, not just a UX. Users don't distrust AI because it's bad — they distrust it because they can't predict when it will be bad. Guardrails, per-card regeneration, and the human-approval boundary in agents are all trust mechanisms. The design job was to make uncertainty feel managed, not hidden.

2. Localization is a product problem, not a translation problem. Supporting both Chinese platforms (WeChat, Xiaohongshu, Douyin, Bilibili) and global ones (Instagram, LinkedIn, X) means "content" means different things in different contexts. The persona system is the design answer — personas encode cultural register, not just language.

What I'd build next

Performance analytics loop: feed published-post metrics back in, and train platform-tone models on what actually performs — guardrails become adaptive.

Team collaboration layer: async comments on output cards, approval workflows, and role-based guardrail editing.

Brand-voice learning: fine-tune on a team's existing content so the AI stops sounding generically "brand-safe" and starts sounding like them.

2The Business

Collov is an AI interior design platform — drag-and-drop tools, 3D visualization, furniture shopping baked in. The pitch was simple: anyone can picture a room before they touch a hammer.

📊
3M+ registered users, 500+ AI-generated templates, 50K+ monthly active. By the numbers Collov was a leading AI interior design platform — and it was bleeding users.

3The Challenge

“1 in every 4 users canceled their subscriptions”

Stakeholder interviews and data analysis pointed to three problems:

  • Same as everyone else. The tool looked and felt like the competition. Users couldn’t tell what they were paying for.
  • Pros locked out, beginners lost. Pro designers hit the ceiling of the simple mode; casual users drowned in advanced controls.
  • Built to look, not to buy. Users loved the renders — then had nowhere to actually buy the furniture in them.

Design Decision
Two modes instead of one compromised UI
72% of churned users were pros who found the tool too basic; new users loved that same simplicity. Instead of meeting in the middle and pleasing no one, I split into two modes: “Home Mode” for casual users, “Pro Mode” for designers. The low entry barrier stays; the pro workflow unlocks. During rollout, tracked subscription drop-off moved from 28.2% to 3.8%. Design’s most direct contribution: segmentation clarity, pro workflow activation, purchase-flow continuity.
Influence & Alignment
Turned “add more features” into a real mode strategy

The hard part wasn’t designing two modes — it was keeping them from splitting into two products. I used stakeholder interviews, churn data, and competitor gaps to push back on a vague “just add more advanced features” ask, and reframe it as a product decision: Home Mode stays simple for casual users; Pro Mode is the path for designers who need CAD import, floor-plan precision, presentation, and purchasing. PM got a sharper positioning story; engineering got a sequenced build.

Trade-offs
Rejected: one UI A single interface would either crush beginners or underserve pros — so I split the Home and Pro entry paths.
Constraint: scope Rather than scatter advanced controls everywhere, I focused Pro Mode on the highest-intent flow: import → generate → edit → present → buy.
Cut from v1 More pro controls could come later. v1 prioritized CAD/photo import, floor-plan precision, AI editing, and purchase continuity.

7Solution: Key Features I Designed

Floor Plan Import
CAD files (DWG/DXF) and room photos. Pros can drop in their existing floor plans instead of redrawing from scratch.
3D Space Generation
2D floor plan → full 3D rendering, powered by AI. Pick room type and style, get a photoreal visualization in minutes.
AI Assistant
Natural language for design edits. Say “add more seats and a display counter for coffee mugs” — the AI regenerates options.
Shopping Cart Integration
Buy furniture straight from the rendered design. The AI suggests similar products from partners (e.g. IKEA); compare and buy in one click.
💭 What I Learned
This project taught me that segmentation isn’t shipping two products — it’s designing one product that bends to who’s using it. Pro Mode wasn’t a feature dump. It came from noticing that pros think in floor plans and measurements, while casual users think in styles and moods — then bridging those two mental models on a shared AI backbone. The tool grows with the user instead of forcing them to pick a side.
